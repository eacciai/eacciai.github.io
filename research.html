Work in Progress

  • Hiding Antiquities: Strategic Market Opacity for Conflict Heritage [Abstract]
    The legal antiquities market operates under persistent suspicion that objects of illicit or uncertain origin circulate through legitimate channels. This paper investigates whether major auction houses strategically adjust the informational content of catalogue entries for antiquities visually resembling art from conflict-affected regions. I assemble a novel dataset of over 45,000 objects sold at leading international auction houses between 1998 and 2025, obtained from auction catalogues. The data combine object images, catalogue text, hammer prices, appraisal values, provenance records, and detailed object characteristics. I deploy deep learning on both images and texts. A convolutional neural network extracts visual embeddings from object photographs, from which I construct a cultural distance proxy, validated against an external benchmark independent of auction house incentives. Sentence embeddings quantify textual opacity in catalogue descriptions. Interacting time-invariant cultural distances with time-varying conflict shocks, I find that informational opacity responds systematically to geopolitical conditions, consistent with strategic information management around objects likely to attract regulatory attention.
  • Art of Deterrence: The Unintended Effects of Public Art [Abstract]
    Place-based interventions can reduce crime, but existing evidence relies exclusively on treatments that alter the physical structure of the urban environment. I estimate whether changing only the visual appearance of a neighborhood can achieve the same goal. I study a large-scale mural initiative in the United States, taking advantage of the staggered rollout of installations. I construct a unit of analysis and trace the spatial decay of the treatment effect. Visibility analysis, exploiting variation in the share of each area that is visible from the installation, provides a direct test of the visual signaling mechanism.

Publications

  • Illicit Shadows: The Cultural Goods Trade Gap for Italy [Abstract] [Paper]
    with M. Belloni, M. Della Giusta, and G. Segre
    Journal of Cultural Economics, 2025.
    The paper investigates how discrepancies in trade data can signal illicit flows of cultural goods. Focusing on Italy, a country highly endowed with cultural heritage and exposed to the risk of illicit trafficking countered by a specialized law enforcement unit, the presence of a consistent gap between the value of cultural goods exported from the country and the value reported by its trading partners over three decades is documented and analyzed. The paper offers two main contributions. First, we estimate a gravity model of trade gaps in four separate categories of cultural goods, each subject to varying degrees of legal protection and vulnerability to illicit trade, incorporating a novel indicator of cultural salience and corruption indicators. Second, we evaluate international policies aimed at curbing illicit trade in cultural goods in terms of their ability to reduce trade gaps. Our findings provide empirical evidence on the relationship between trade gaps and illicit markets for cultural objects, and confirm the role of interest in Italian heritage as a driving factor. Archaeological property is further analyzed separately before and after 2010 to show that the apparent increase of Italian trade may partly result from the Arab Springs' consequences on destruction and looting of cultural heritage. Institutional enforcement through the UNESCO and UNIDROIT conventions is partially effective in reducing illicit trade.
